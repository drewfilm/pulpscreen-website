Co-Productions

How to leverage the European Convention, bilateral treaties, and incentive stacking to build your capital stack for feature films.

Why Co-Produce in Europe?

Well-structured bilateral co-productions can assemble 35–50% of budget in soft money before equity is even approached. The European Convention on Cinematographic Co-production covers 43 countries under a single multilateral framework. Each co-producing territory's spend qualifies for that country's national incentive — meaning your film is treated as a "national" production in every participating country simultaneously.

Step-by-Step Process

Identify Partner Territories

Map your creative and financial needs against territories with strong incentives, crew bases, and locations. Consider language, treaty access, and Eurimages eligibility.

Check Treaty Coverage

Determine whether the European Convention applies or if you need a bilateral treaty. The Convention's 2018 revision reduced the multilateral minimum contribution to 5% (from 10%).

Structure the Split

Each partner must contribute minimum 5–20% (varies by treaty). Creative and technical contributions must balance financial stakes. The minority partner's 20% threshold is critical.

Apply for National Status

Submit to each country's film body for "Approved Co-production Status." Official co-productions automatically pass cultural tests in most territories (e.g. UK, France).

Stack the Incentives

Each territory's qualifying spend unlocks its national incentive independently. UK AVEC (25%) + Ireland Section 481 (32–40%) + Eurimages funding = major capital stack.

Apply to Eurimages

The Council of Europe's co-production fund adds another capital layer. Qualifying European co-productions can access grants up to €500K. Frequently overlooked by non-EU producers.

Secure Pre-Approval

Most programs require certification before principal photography. Apply 4–6 weeks minimum before shooting begins. Late applications risk disqualifying spend.

Audit & Claim

Post-production, submit audited expenditure reports to each territory. Budget for qualified CPAs in each jurisdiction. Audit failures can reduce your effective rebate by 10–20%.

Key Considerations

Important Notes for Feature Film Producers

The 20% Rule: The minimum financial and creative contribution required from the minority partner. Fall below this, and you're classified as a service production with no rights to the local incentive.

Rights Allocation: Co-production treaties require that rights are split in proportion to each partner's financial contribution. This affects your distribution strategy.

Residency Requirements: Some territories require the producer or key personnel to reside in-country during production. Verify before committing.

Cash Flow Reality: Incentives are paid post-completion and audit. If you need upfront liquidity, you'll finance against the expected credit and pay fees. Model realistically.
